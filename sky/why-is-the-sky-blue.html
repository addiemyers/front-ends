Chapter 1

Sunlight contains every color

The sun sends out white light — but white light isn't really just one thing. It's a blend of every visible color, each one a different wavelength of electromagnetic radiation traveling at the speed of light toward Earth.

Pass sunlight through a prism and those colors split apart, forming the familiar rainbow spectrum from deep violet to rich red.

Chapter 2

The atmosphere is full of tiny obstacles

Our atmosphere is packed with billions of tiny gas molecules — mostly nitrogen (N₂) and oxygen (O₂). These molecules are unimaginably small, far smaller than the wavelengths of visible light.

When a photon of light slams into one of these molecules, something fascinating happens: the photon gets absorbed for a brief instant, then fired back out in a random direction. This is called Rayleigh scattering, named after Lord Rayleigh who figured it out in the 1870s.

⚛️

Nitrogen (N₂)

Makes up ~78% of Earth's atmosphere. These tiny molecules are the main scatterers of blue light.

💨

Oxygen (O₂)

About 21% of the atmosphere. Also scatters blue light, contributing to that gorgeous cerulean dome above us.

🔵

Rayleigh Scattering

Shorter wavelengths scatter much more. Blue light scatters ~5.5× more than red light of the same intensity.

Chapter 3 — Interactive

Shorter wavelengths scatter more

Here's the key insight: the amount of scattering depends very strongly on the wavelength of the light. Shorter (bluer) wavelengths scatter far more than longer (redder) ones.

Chapter 5 — The plot twist

Wait — why not violet?

Here's a fantastic question: if shorter wavelengths scatter more, and violet light (~380 nm) is shorter than blue (~450 nm), shouldn't the sky look violet?

🌞

Less violet to start

The sun emits significantly less violet light than blue light. Our sun's spectrum peaks in the green-yellow region.

👁️

Human eye sensitivity

Our eyes have three color receptors and are much less sensitive to violet than to blue, green, or red.

🌌

Violet absorbed high up

Much of the incoming violet light gets absorbed by ozone in the upper atmosphere before it reaches us.

The result? Blue wins the visible sky competition — the most intense scattered light that our eyes are tuned to perceive.

Chapter 6

And why are sunsets red?

At sunset, sunlight must travel through a much thicker slice of atmosphere to reach you. The blue light scatters away long before it arrives. Only the longer wavelengths — reds, oranges, and pinks — survive the journey.

🔵 blue scattered away
🔴 red survives →

The longer the path through the atmosphere, the more blue is filtered out — which is why the most dramatic sunsets happen when there's dust or smoke in the air, adding even more scattering particles.
